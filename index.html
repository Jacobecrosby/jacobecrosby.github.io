Bio

Hello! My name is Dr. Jacob Crosby and I'm a physicist who received his PhD from the High Energy Physics team at Oklahoma State University. My curiosity has carved my career in physics! I focus on applying industry latest tech tools to redefine physics techniques in hopes to discover new secrets of our universe. I also enjoy using these skills to create fun, tangible projects. I'm a task-driven problem solver that aims to innovate with my curiosity always looking for new opportunities!

Research

Currently leading a team of software engineers within the Hardware Development Lab at Oklahoma State University to ensure hardware production and testing for newly designed parts that are vital to the upgarde of the ATLAS detector which will be operational in 2029. The team has created a comprehensive full-stack environment, which includes a robust back-end infrastructure and a uniquely tailored front-end UI to streamline processes for the production testing team. This work includes adapting a novel anomaly detection technique using machine learning to find visual defects within production components. Primary contributor of developing a new model agnostistic physics analysis technique using unsupervised machine learning to discover new physics within the ATLAS detector, located at CERN, Switzerland. Developed the first Deep Learning identification ML models to tag particles for the new High-Luminosity Large Hadron Collider (HL-LHC) that is currently under construction and will be operating in 2039. I worked as the Physics Validation contact for the Flavour Tagging team on the ATLAS experiment from Feb. 2023 - March. 2024 and served as Diversity and Equity Graduate Representitive for the Oklahoma State University Physics Department.

Projects

Currently, I'm designing a pocket sized scalable particle detector used to detect cosmic rays! With its prototype being designed in Altium Designer. The finsihed product will be available to purchase, so stay tuned!

A while back I worked at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), formally known as the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory (NSCL). Here, I had the opportunity to work on a few projects. A notable project I created for physics outreach was to create a virtual reality application that puts the user inside a simulated version of a Time Project Chamber (TPC). This application used real-world data taken by the Spirit TPC, located in Japan. Two events were simulated, one was using a nuclear collision on a target and the other used an observed cosmic ray.
